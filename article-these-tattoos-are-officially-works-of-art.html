These Tattoos Are Officially Works of Art

By Dan J - June 12, 2019

These Tattoos Are Officially Works of Art

Introduction and Context

Within contemporary visual culture, the status and valuation of tattoo art has undergone profound transformation. Historically relegated to margins of respectability and often associated with criminality, marginalization, and subcultural rebellion, tattoo practice has increasingly been recognized as legitimate artistic medium deserving institutional validation and critical engagement. The claim that tattoos are "officially works of art" represents recognition of a cultural shift that has been developing gradually over decades but which accelerated significantly in the early twenty-first century. This shift reflects broader transformations in artistic legitimacy, the relationship between fine art and popular cultural practices, and how institutions of art world recognize and validate artistic work. Understanding tattoos as legitimate artistic practice requires examining the history of tattoo tradition, transformations in tattoo practice itself, and how institutional and cultural attitudes toward tattoos have evolved.

Tattoo practice extends back millennia in human cultural history. Archaeological evidence reveals that tattooing practices existed in ancient Egypt, Rome, China, Japan, and numerous other cultural contexts. In many traditional and non-Western cultures, tattooing has carried significant cultural, spiritual, and social meaning, serving as markers of status, spiritual protection, cultural identity, or rites of passage. However, the particular history of tattoo practice in contemporary Western contexts has been shaped by colonial and imperialist encounters, in which Western cultures encountered tattooing practices they deemed "primitive" or "savage," creating associations between tattoos and perceived racial otherness. These colonial associations, combined with tattoos' adoption by marginalized urban subcultures and criminal populations in Western contexts, resulted in tattoos being regarded with suspicion and contempt by respectable Western society for much of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries.

Historical Evolution of Tattoo Practice

Tattoo practice in Western contexts has undergone significant transformation since the late twentieth century. Early modern Western tattooing, practiced primarily among sailors, military personnel, prisoners, and circus performers, involved relatively limited technical sophistication, often employing hand-made instruments and improvised inks. Tattooing was frequently practiced by individuals with minimal formal artistic training, and the technical limitations of early methods constrained the artistic possibilities available to practitioners. The emergence of the electric tattoo machine in the late nineteenth century, while enabling more efficient production, did not immediately transform tattoo as artistic practice. Rather, the democratization of tattooing that the machine enabled meant that tattoo production proliferated without necessarily elevating artistic standards or cultural prestige.

The professionalization of tattoo practice began gradually during the late twentieth century, particularly from the 1970s onward. Individual tattoo artists, recognizing that their work deserved recognition as legitimate artistic practice, began implementing more rigorous standards for artistic training, equipment quality, and hygiene practices. Pioneering contemporary tattoo artists such as Ed Hardy and Lyle Tuttle began combining traditional tattoo practices with contemporary artistic sensibilities, drawing on fine art traditions and developing innovative technical approaches. These artists worked deliberately to establish tattoo as respectable artistic medium, often incorporating formal artistic training alongside traditional tattoo apprenticeships. They developed distinctive artistic aesthetics that incorporated color sophistication, fine detail, and conceptual depth previously rare in Western tattoo practice. These practitioners, through their professional dedication and artistic seriousness, began transforming tattoo culture and establishing tattooing as legitimate artistic practice.

The Contemporary Tattoo Industry and Artistic Standards

Contemporary tattoo practice has become increasingly professionalized and artistically sophisticated. Serious contemporary tattoo artists undergo extensive training, often combining traditional apprenticeships with formal artistic education. Major contemporary tattoo practitioners frequently possess formal arts education and engage with tattoo practice as serious artistic investigation rather than commercial enterprise. The finest contemporary tattoo artists work within distinctive artistic traditions and styles—photorealism, color work, black and gray portraiture, botanical illustration, geometric abstraction—approaching their work with artistic seriousness equivalent to painters, sculptors, or other fine artists. The level of technical skill required to execute sophisticated contemporary tattoo work has increased substantially, with contemporary practitioners developing innovative techniques and employing specialized equipment that allows unprecedented control and precision.

The economic structure of contemporary tattoo practice has also transformed. Rather than being a marginalized, poorly compensated activity, successful contemporary tattoo artists can command substantial fees for their work and achieve significant financial success. Major contemporary tattoo artists maintain waiting lists stretching months or years, with clients willing to pay substantial sums for work by artists they deem exceptional. This economic valuation reflects broader cultural recognition of tattoo work as serious artistic practice. The most accomplished contemporary tattoo artists receive commissions from celebrities, collectors, and institutions willing to recognize their work as valuable and worthy of significant investment.

Institutional Recognition and Legitimacy

The shift toward institutional recognition of tattoos as legitimate artistic practice has developed gradually but steadily. Museums and art institutions, long dismissive of tattoo practice, have begun acquiring and exhibiting tattoo-related work. In 2015, the British Museum organized a major exhibition titled "Tattoo: British Tattoos and Tattooists" that examined the history and contemporary practice of tattooing in Britain through extensive collection of artifacts, photographs, and artist interviews. This exhibition, presented at one of the world's most prestigious institutions, represented significant institutional validation of tattoo as worthy of serious artistic and historical examination. Subsequent major exhibitions at other significant institutions have followed, establishing pattern of increasing institutional engagement with tattoo as legitimate artistic and cultural practice.

Universities and art schools have increasingly incorporated tattoo practice within formal artistic training. Some of the world's most prestigious art institutions now offer courses in tattoo history, culture, and practice, and some institutions have begun incorporating tattoo practice itself within formal curricula. This institutional recognition reflects growing appreciation for tattoo as serious artistic medium and acknowledgment that understanding contemporary artistic practice requires engagement with tattoo traditions. The development of tattoo studies as emerging academic field has produced scholarship examining tattoo history, aesthetics, cultural meanings, and social significance, further establishing tattoo as legitimate subject of serious intellectual inquiry.

Artistic Merit and Aesthetic Sophistication

Contemporary tattoo art has achieved aesthetic sophistication and artistic complexity rivaling or exceeding many other artistic practices. Contemporary tattoo artists develop distinctive artistic voices, create innovative technical approaches, and engage with artistic and conceptual traditions with seriousness equivalent to practitioners of other visual arts. The constraints inherent to tattoo practice—the necessity of working on living bodies, the permanence of the medium, the limitations of skin as surface—require extraordinary skill and artistic vision to address successfully. Skilled contemporary tattoo artists overcome these constraints to create work of genuine beauty, sophistication, and conceptual depth.

Photorealistic tattooing, which has emerged as dominant style in contemporary practice, demands technical mastery approaching that of representational painters. Achieving convincing photographic representation on skin surface, accounting for skin curvature and movement, requires extraordinary technical control and artistic understanding. Similarly, color work in contemporary tattooing, increasingly sophisticated in its chromatic relationships and tonal complexity, represents artistic investigation equivalent to color painting. Geometric and abstract tattoo work demonstrates conceptual sophistication and formal innovation. Fine-line work, which involves executing extremely delicate marks on skin surface, requires extraordinary precision and control. The diversity of contemporary tattoo aesthetics and the sophistication of contemporary practice demonstrates that tattoos are not simple decoration but rather serious artistic medium capable of complex aesthetic and conceptual investigation.

Collector Interest and Market Valuation

The emergence of serious collector interest in tattoo art further validates its status as legitimate artistic practice. Collectors increasingly view tattoos as significant artistic investments. The most accomplished contemporary tattoo artists maintain international reputations and command prices for their work that reflect serious valuation. Collectors are willing to travel internationally to work with specific artists they deem exceptional, and they value pieces by significant contemporary tattoo practitioners with equivalent seriousness to fine art collecting. This market valuation reflects broader cultural recognition of tattoo as serious artistic practice worthy of significant financial investment.

Social Significance and Personal Meaning

Beyond formal artistic considerations, tattoos remain profoundly significant in terms of personal meaning, identity expression, and social communication. Tattoos allow individuals to inscribe meaning on their bodies, creating permanent records of significant moments, relationships, beliefs, and identities. This significance extends far beyond mere aesthetic ornamentation. For many individuals, tattoos represent crucial expressions of agency, identity, and personal meaning. The permanence of tattoos—their refusal to be temporary or ephemeral—makes them fundamentally different from most aesthetic practices. Tattooing is therefore a practice that combines artistic sophistication with profound personal and social meaning in ways that distinguish it from many other artistic practices.

The Future of Tattoo Practice

As institutional recognition of tattoos as legitimate artistic practice has increased, this has paradoxically created certain tensions. The professionalization and institutionalization of tattoo practice, while validating it as serious artistic medium, has potentially removed some of the transgressive and countercultural dimensions that historically characterized tattoo culture. This raises interesting questions about what is gained and lost as tattoo practice becomes increasingly respectable and incorporated within mainstream artistic institutions. Despite these tensions, the trajectory of tattoo practice in contemporary culture seems clear: tattoos are increasingly recognized as legitimate artistic medium deserving serious critical engagement, institutional support, and cultural prestige. The claim that tattoos are "officially works of art" represents not merely a statement of current status but recognition of transformation that has already fundamentally changed how tattoos are valued and understood within contemporary culture.
