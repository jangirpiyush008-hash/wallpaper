Paul Hallows’ Patterns Show Common City Infrastructure

By Jovana A - August 28, 2019

Paul Hallows' artistic practice focuses on detailed, meticulously rendered illustrations and patterns that celebrate the often-overlooked visual beauty of common city infrastructure elements. His work finds artistic merit and visual interest in features of the urban landscape that most people pass by without conscious notice—utility boxes, street signs, manhole covers, building facades, and other functional elements of urban design. By elevating these everyday objects and features to the status of subjects worthy of artistic attention and detailed study, Hallows encourages viewers to reconsider their relationship with the urban environments they navigate daily. His illustrations demonstrate exceptional technical skill and an almost obsessive attention to detail, with each element of the infrastructure carefully observed and meticulously rendered.

The patterns that emerge from these urban elements often possess an unintended beauty that Hallows' work makes visible and worthy of aesthetic contemplation. His color choices and compositional approaches transform what might seem like mundane subject matter into visually compelling artworks that reward close examination. His practice contributes to a broader movement in contemporary art that seeks to find beauty and meaning in overlooked or dismissed aspects of everyday experience. By focusing on the visual character of urban infrastructure, Hallows' work also implicitly comments on design, functionality, and the relationship between aesthetic and practical concerns in the built environment.

His work appeals to those interested in urban design, typography, pattern, and the everyday beauty of city life. Through his detailed illustrations of city infrastructure patterns, Paul Hallows has created a body of work that challenges viewers to see their surroundings with fresh eyes and appreciation.
