Amy Feigley-Lee’s Abstract Sculptures Made from Vintage Wallpaper Play with Viewer’s...

By Kate A - June 25, 2024

Contemporary sculptural practice frequently engages with unconventional materials and found objects, recognizing that traditional art materials like marble and bronze need not define the medium. Amy Feigley-Lee has developed a distinctive sculptural practice utilizing vintage wallpaper and other discarded decorative materials, creating abstract sculptural forms that simultaneously reference domestic interior design traditions while achieving formal sophistication and conceptual rigor. Her work demonstrates how materials considered disposable gain new significance when engaged artistically.

Amy Feigley-Lee's background encompasses formal sculpture training combined with growing interest in vernacular materials, domestic aesthetics, and the challenge of creating conceptually sophisticated work from materials associated with quotidian spaces. Rather than viewing wallpaper as craft material suited only to interior decoration, Feigley-Lee recognized sculptural potential. The decision to work with vintage wallpaper specifically reflected interest in materials carrying historical association and aesthetic value from their original contexts while remaining accessible and inexpensive. Vintage wallpaper carries visual and cultural information about particular historical moments.

The technical methodology involves transforming flat wallpaper into three-dimensional sculptural forms through cutting, folding, layering, and assembling techniques. The process requires understanding how two-dimensional patterns interact when reformed into dimensional structures, how visual effects created through color and pattern function when distributed across complex three-dimensional forms. Feigley-Lee has developed particular sophistication in structural engineering—creating stable forms from materials not traditionally considered sculptural.

The creative process emphasizes play, experimentation, and discovery—rather than beginning with predetermined sculptural forms, Feigley-Lee frequently begins with material exploration. How does this particular wallpaper pattern behave when folded? What visual effects emerge when patterns are reversed or inverted? This experimental approach allows the material itself to suggest formal possibilities, with the sculptor remaining responsive to what the material reveals. The dialogue between artist intention and material properties creates sculptures that feel discovered rather than imposed.

The resulting sculptures range from relatively modest table-top scale to room-sized installations transforming exhibition spaces. Some emphasize pattern and color interaction. Others are more minimalist, using simple forms and limited color to achieve formal elegance. Many engage directly with viewer interaction—sculptures designed to reveal different visual effects from different viewpoints, or pieces that change appearance dramatically depending on lighting conditions.

The cultural impact reflects the work's role in expanding recognition of unconventional materials as worthy of serious artistic attention and demonstrating that sophistication can emerge from engagement with humble, domestic materials. The work appeals to audiences interested in recycled materials, sustainability, and the transformation of discarded objects into valued artistic works. Museums and contemporary art institutions have increasingly recognized wallpaper-based sculpture as significant artistic practice.

Amy's creative philosophy emphasizes respect for materials' histories and properties, playfulness and experimentation as legitimate artistic approaches, and the belief that artistic significance need not correlate with material preciousness or expense. Rather than treating found materials as compromised alternatives to traditional art materials, the work celebrates what becomes possible through engagement with abundant, accessible materials.

The journey to recognition involved participation in contemporary art exhibitions, features in contemporary art and design publications, and growing visibility through social media and online platforms. Feigley-Lee's sculptures began circulating through contemporary art communities, building recognition among audiences interested in expanded sculptural practice. Museum exhibitions, acquisition by major collections, and speaking engagements positioned Feigley-Lee as significant voice in contemporary sculpture.
