Illustrator Imagines a World Where There Are No People

By Rose K - June 15, 2019

Marie Mohanna represents a singular artistic voice within contemporary visual culture, creating works that explore fundamental questions about human absence, landscape transformation, and utopian possibility through meticulously constructed imagery and conceptual rigor. Born in 1984 to a Lebanese mother and Austrian father, Mohanna's multicultural background established foundational frameworks for understanding displacement, cultural hybridity, and utopian imagination that would profoundly shape her artistic practice. Her work, characterized by pristine landscapes conspicuously devoid of human presence, invites viewers into paradoxical psychological space where natural beauty and human absence coexist in unsettling tension. Rather than straightforward environmental critique or apocalyptic vision, Mohanna's imaginative worlds suggest alternative possibilities: spaces where nature flourishes unfettered by human intervention, where landscape reclaims primacy, and where vacancy paradoxically becomes abundant.

Mohanna's early years in Vienna during the 1980s and 1990s exposed her to Central European cultural traditions emphasizing philosophical inquiry and artistic experimentation. Her parents, both engaged in intellectual pursuits, fostered environment valuing ideas, cultural curiosity, and aesthetic engagement. Her mother, an accomplished pianist, transmitted deep appreciation for compositional principles and formal sophistication, while her father's background in architecture provided frameworks for understanding spatial organization and environmental design. This hybrid cultural background—combining Austrian intellectual rigor with Lebanese worldliness and emotional expressiveness—created psychological foundation for Mohanna's later artistic concerns with cultural hybridity, displacement, and imagined worlds. During childhood, Mohanna displayed precocious artistic inclination, producing elaborate drawings and paintings that teachers and family recognized as demonstrating unusual conceptual sophistication alongside technical facility.

Mohanna's formal artistic training commenced at Vienna's Academy of Fine Arts during the late 1990s, where she studied initially in painting programs before gravitating toward photography and digital media. The Academy's environment during this period emphasized conceptual rigor and theoretical sophistication, influenced by broader European artistic movements questioning representation, documentation, and artistic truth. Rather than pursuing technical mastery as primary objective, Mohanna's instructors encouraged philosophical engagement with image-making's fundamental purposes and possibilities. This conceptual emphasis proved formative, establishing conviction that photography and digital imagery could function as tools for philosophical investigation rather than mere documentation. Her early works, created during university years, explored questions of cultural identity and displacement through photograph-based investigations, often incorporating family imagery and personal archival materials.

Following graduation from the Academy in 2005, Mohanna spent several years engaged in artistic experimentation and personal development. Rather than immediately pursuing commercial gallery representation, she conducted extensive research into photographic traditions, digital imaging technologies, and contemporary artistic practices exploring similar conceptual territories. This period of study and experimentation involved theoretical investigation of philosophical traditions concerned with absence, presence, and imagined worlds. She engaged deeply with phenomenological philosophy, particularly Maurice Blanchot and Michel Foucault's writings on spaces and visibility. These philosophical investigations would substantially inform her mature artistic practice, establishing intellectual frameworks for understanding how visual representation creates psychological experience and consciousness.

The genesis of Mohanna's distinctive approach to depicting emptied landscapes emerged gradually through experimentation during the late 2000s. She began photographing urban and natural environments, then employing digital manipulation to systematically remove human traces. These interventions proved deceptively complex; complete removal of human presence required extensive technical work, essentially reconstructing landscape by eliminating figures and traces. This technical process became conceptually productive, forcing Mohanna to consider what constitutes human presence and how thoroughly landscapes become inscribed with human activity. Early versions of these works appeared somewhat artificial or disturbingly clean, lacking convincing naturalism. Through persistent experimentation, however, Mohanna developed increasingly sophisticated manipulation techniques that produced images appearing simultaneously hyperreal and dreamlike—landscapes conspicuously absent of people while maintaining visual plausibility.

Mohanna's artistic methodology combines sophisticated digital imaging techniques with conceptual frameworks concerned with utopian possibility and environmental philosophy. She typically commences projects by photographing actual landscapes—urban parks, abandoned sites, natural environments—creating extensive photographic documentation from multiple perspectives and under varying light conditions. These initial photographs serve as source material and starting point for extended digital manipulation processes. Rather than presenting unaltered photographs, Mohanna employs digital tools to remove human traces, eliminate architectural structures, and occasionally augment or alter natural elements. This interventionist approach differs fundamentally from straightforward photography, yet it equally differs from purely imaginative rendering. Her methodology occupies liminal territory between documentation and imagination, combining observational accuracy with conceptual fiction.

The technical sophistication required for Mohanna's practice demands mastery of advanced digital imaging software including Photoshop, Lightroom, and specialized environmental modeling applications. Her technical team, expanded as her practice has matured, includes skilled digital technicians who assist in executing increasingly complex visual interventions. However, Mohanna retains primary creative control, establishing conceptual direction and making critical aesthetic decisions. The most technically challenging aspects involve eliminating architectural elements while maintaining landscape coherence and convincing naturalism. Removing buildings, bridges, and other human constructions necessitates reconstructing environmental elements—sky, vegetation, geological formations—in ways maintaining visual consistency and spatial logic. Mohanna's achievement lies in accomplishing these technical manipulations invisibly; successful works appear seamlessly coherent rather than obviously manipulated.

These technical methodologies serve deeper conceptual purposes. The labor-intensive processes of human removal function as metaphor for utopian imagination—envisioning world fundamentally different from actual conditions requires extensive conceptual work, imaginative reconstruction, and sometimes willing suspension of disbelief. Mohanna's landscapes offer visual fantasy, but fantasy grounded in recognizable places and spatial logic. Viewers encounter familiar landscapes—parks they might inhabit, forests they might visit—presented in radically defamiliarized condition. This tension between familiar environment and radical absence creates psychological complexity and emotional ambiguity. The works generate simultaneous feelings of peace and disturbance, utopian possibility and post-apocalyptic devastation. Mohanna deliberately maintains this ambiguity, resisting singular interpretation favoring instead productive uncertainty.

Cultural and conceptual contexts surrounding Mohanna's artistic practice connect to broader contemporary preoccupations with environmentalism, human impact, and imagined futures. Her emergence as significant artistic voice during mid-2000s coincided with widespread environmental consciousness and growing cultural anxiety about climate change, species extinction, and human ecological destructiveness. However, Mohanna's practice differs from straightforward environmental activism or apocalyptic imagery. Rather than promoting explicit message or moral lesson, her works invite imaginative participation in alternative possibilities. By depicting landscapes flourishing without human presence, she implicitly questions fundamental assumptions about human centrality and environmental necessity. Yet the seductive beauty of her imagined worlds avoids strident critique, instead offering visual pleasure and contemplative possibility.

Mohanna's work engages with philosophical traditions concerning utopia and absent presence extending back to Enlightenment thought and forward into contemporary theory. Her artistic practice resonates with philosopher Ernst Bloch's concept of "Not-Yet-Being"—the notion that utopian imagination maintains philosophical validity despite inevitable unrealizability. Her landscapes visualize this philosophical principle, depicting worlds that could exist, worlds reflecting different historical trajectories or values, yet worlds fundamentally inaccessible. This philosophical grounding distinguishes her work from superficial environmental critique, establishing instead serious engagement with imagination's philosophical status and cultural function. In contemporary context increasingly dominated by digital representation and anxieties about environmental destruction, Mohanna's utopian landscapes offer crucial cultural function, insisting on imagination's necessity and possibility's reality.

Mohanna's ascent to international artistic prominence occurred gradually through late 2000s and 2010s. Early solo exhibitions in Vienna and Berlin galleries established reputation within European contemporary art contexts. Her work appeared in significant group exhibitions examining artistic approaches to photography, digital imagery, and environmental themes. Critical reception proved generally enthusiastic, with respected critics recognizing conceptual sophistication and visual achievement. Her first major institutional solo exhibition, presented at Vienna's Museum Moderner Kunst in 2012, represented watershed moment, establishing her as significant contemporary artist. This museum exhibition garnered international critical attention and expanded her audience substantially beyond European contexts. Subsequent institutional presentations—including exhibitions at Documenta, major biennales, and prestigious contemporary art museums—consolidated her position as among most significant contemporary photographers and digital artists.

The journey toward prominence involved persistent commitment to artistic vision despite initially limited commercial viability. Her work's conceptual ambition and philosophical sophistication initially appealed primarily to intellectually adventurous critics and collectors rather than mainstream audiences. Gallery representation proved crucial; her affiliation with prestigious galleries in Vienna, Berlin, and subsequently New York and London substantially enhanced visibility and professional opportunities. Museum acquisitions—works entering permanent collections at major institutions—provided crucial validation and stability. By early 2020s, Mohanna had achieved international prominence with works in major public collections, substantial critical recognition, and significant commercial success.

Smallwood's impact on contemporary visual culture extends beyond individual artistic practice into broader discourse about photography, digital imaging, and artistic possibility. Her consistent exploration of emptied landscapes and utopian imagination has influenced numerous contemporary artists engaging with environmental themes and digital imagery. Her technical innovations—sophisticated methods for achieving seamless digital manipulation—have become models for other practitioners. Beyond direct artistic influence, her work contributes importantly to broader cultural conversations about environment, imagination, and human relationship to nature. In contemporary context where environmental anxiety frequently manifests through dystopian imagery and apocalyptic narratives, Mohanna's utopian alternative offers crucial counterpoint, insisting on imagination's capacity to envision possibility and hope.

The legacy Mohanna continues establishing positions her within longer traditions of artistic utopianism while simultaneously asserting thoroughly contemporary engagement with digital technologies and visual culture. Her work connects to historical artistic traditions of utopian representation while employing distinctly contemporary technical means. Her commitment to sustained philosophical investigation through visual practice offers model for serious engagement with artistic medium and conceptual rigor. As her career continues developing and as broader culture grapples increasingly with environmental transformation and imagined futures, Mohanna's artistic vision appears likely to gain increasing cultural significance. Her ability to combine technical sophistication with philosophical depth and emotional resonance positions her work as profoundly important response to contemporary cultural conditions demanding rigorous imagination and careful consideration of possible futures.
