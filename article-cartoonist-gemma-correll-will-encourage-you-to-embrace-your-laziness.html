Cartoonist Gemma Correll Will Encourage You to Embrace Your Laziness

By Rose K - August 26, 2019

Gemma Correll is a cartoonist and illustrator whose work celebrates and encourages embracing laziness, procrastination, and a general lack of productivity, positioning these behaviors not as personal failures but as valid and human responses to the pressures of contemporary society. Her cartoons and illustrations feature relatable characters and situations depicting the universal experiences of avoiding work, seeking comfort, and choosing rest over accomplishment. Correll's humor is warm and non-judgmental, creating characters and scenarios that readers recognize in themselves, fostering a sense of community and mutual understanding among those who struggle with conventional productivity expectations. Her work operates as a form of gentle resistance to productivity culture, the pervasive belief that a human's value and worth is determined by their economic output or accomplishments.

By treating laziness and procrastination with humor and compassion rather than shame or judgment, Correll's work offers a counternarrative to the toxic productivity culture that dominates contemporary workplaces and social media. Her illustrations often feature animals and humanoid characters in situations involving napping, snacking, avoidance, and generally doing nothing, rendered in a charming, accessible illustration style that makes the work immediately appealing and shareable. The text accompanying her illustrations often includes pithy, quotable observations about human nature, rest, and the limitations of pushing oneself to constant productivity. Her work has achieved significant popularity, particularly among younger audiences navigating the expectations and stresses of contemporary employment and academic contexts.

Her books and social media presence have created a community of people who feel validated in their struggles with conventional productivity expectations. Through her cartoons celebrating laziness, Correll has contributed to a broader cultural conversation about work, rest, and the relationship between productivity and human worth.
